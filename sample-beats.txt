SCREENPLAY CARDS — SAMPLE BEATS
Demo project: "The Last Forecast"
================================
A meteorologist discovers the weather models have been predicting a catastrophic storm
that the government is suppressing. She has 48 hours to warn the coast.


HOW TO USE THIS FILE
--------------------
For each beat below, the FRONT is the one-line title that goes on the front of the card.
The BACK NOTES are what you type on the back of the card.
The ACT HEADER cards are excluded from scene numbering (click the SC. number to toggle it off).

================================
BEAT 1
================================

FRONT:  Elena checks the models — something is wrong

BACK NOTES:
ELENA VASQUEZ (38), lead meteorologist at the National Weather Center, runs her
morning data pull. Every model — all six of them — is showing the same anomaly.
A pressure system that shouldn't exist. She flags it as a possible error and
moves on. But it stays with her.

Visual: the glow of six monitors at 4am. Coffee going cold.
Tone: quiet unease. The calm before.


================================
BEAT 2
================================

FRONT:  Her supervisor kills the alert before it goes out

BACK NOTES:
Elena submits the standard coastal advisory. DIRECTOR HOLT (55, former Navy,
political appointee) calls her into his office within minutes. Pulls the alert.
Tells her the models are running a known calibration error. She should stand down.

She doesn't believe him. He doesn't care.

Note: establish that Holt is not the villain — he's just scared and following orders.
The real pressure is coming from above him.


================================
BEAT 3
================================

FRONT:  Elena finds the fisherman's footage

BACK NOTES:
Digging through citizen reports, Elena finds a video uploaded by MARCO (60s),
a commercial fisherman 80 miles offshore. The water is doing something the
models predicted — three days ago — before Holt pulled her data.

The models aren't wrong. Someone knew. Someone pulled them anyway.

This is the moment she stops being a scientist and starts being a threat.


================================
BEAT 4
================================

FRONT:  She reaches out to her ex — a journalist — and immediately regrets it

BACK NOTES:
DANIEL PARK (41), investigative reporter, her ex-husband. They ended badly.
She calls him because she doesn't know who else to trust.

He's skeptical. She sounds unhinged even to herself. But he agrees to meet.

Subtext: she's also testing whether she can trust her own judgment.
The relationship history is present but not the point — don't overplay it.

BEAT 5
================================

FRONT:  The data disappears from the servers

BACK NOTES:
Elena goes back to pull her original models as evidence. They're gone.
Not archived. Deleted. The logs show she deleted them herself — which she
absolutely did not do.

Someone inside the Weather Center is cleaning up after her.

This beat should feel procedural and cold, not dramatic. No confrontation.
Just a blinking cursor where her life's work used to be.


================================
BEAT 6
================================

FRONT:  Daniel finds the paper trail — a FEMA contract two years old

BACK NOTES:
Daniel digs into it from his end. Finds a FEMA contract for "Coastal Resilience
Modeling" awarded to a private firm called Meridian Analytics. The contract
includes a non-disclosure clause about predictive weather data during election
cycles.

The storm isn't the story. The cover-up of the storm is the story.

This is the turn. Now they're not just trying to warn people — they're up against
something institutional.


================================
BEAT 7
================================

FRONT:  Elena drives to Marco's dock — he's already gone

BACK NOTES:
She drives four hours to find Marco, the fisherman. His boat is in the slip.
His truck is in the lot. His wife hasn't heard from him since yesterday.

Don't make this a thriller beat. Make it sad. Make it real.
A missing old man and a worried woman in a harbor parking lot.

Elena understands for the first time that people could die — not from the storm,
but from knowing about the storm.


================================
BEAT 8
================================

FRONT:  She goes on the record — Daniel publishes

BACK NOTES:
Elena makes the decision to go public even though it will end her career.
Daniel publishes the story with what they have — incomplete, but enough.

The moment of publication should be quiet, not triumphant. They're sitting
in his car outside a diner. He hits send. They eat bad pie and wait.

The storm is now 31 hours out.


================================
BEAT 9
================================

FRONT:  The story gets buried — then breaks anyway

BACK NOTES:
The story goes up. Gets suppressed on social. Picked up by one local affiliate
in Savannah who doesn't know they're supposed to ignore it.

That affiliate runs it. Then another. Then the wire services.

The government issues a statement calling Elena's data "unverified and alarmist."
She watches it on her phone from a motel room. Holt calls her. He sounds tired.
Not angry. Tired.

"You should have let me protect you," he says.

BEAT 11
================================

FRONT:  Holt comes to her — off the record

BACK NOTES:
Holt drives to the motel himself. No security. No phone.
He sits in the parking lot in his personal car for ten minutes before
she notices him and comes outside.

He tells her Meridian Analytics flagged her models six months ago.
He was told a public warning would trigger a financial panic in coastal
real estate markets before the election. He was told to wait.
He waited. He shouldn't have.

He's not asking for forgiveness. He's asking her to understand.
She doesn't answer. He drives away.

This scene only works if we've been fair to Holt. He's not evil.
He made a calculation. The calculation was wrong.


================================
BEAT 12
================================

FRONT:  Elena finds Marco — he's alive, just scared

BACK NOTES:
Marco didn't disappear. He ran.

He's at his sister's place two counties inland. He saw something out on
the water that frightened him — not the storm. Men on a government
vessel taking water temperature readings in exactly the sector Elena
had flagged. He knew what it meant. He drove away from the coast
and didn't look back.

He gives Elena a recorded video statement on his phone.
His hands shake the whole time.

This beat is relief — but only partial. They have evidence.
They're still alone with it.


================================
BEAT 13
================================

FRONT:  Daniel's editor kills the follow-up story

BACK NOTES:
Daniel goes back to his editor with the new material — Holt's admission,
Marco's video, the Meridian contract timeline.

The editor kills it. Legal has concerns. The publisher has concerns.
Daniel is told to take a few days.

He and Elena sit in his car again. Different parking lot. Same feeling.

"What do we do with this?" she asks.
"We find someone who isn't afraid of it," he says.

They don't have anyone in mind yet. That's the honest answer.

BEAT 14
================================

FRONT:  The Savannah affiliate runs the full story — names included

BACK NOTES:
The local affiliate that broke the first story agrees to run everything.
Small station. Nothing to lose. Reporter is twenty-six years old and
has no idea she's about to have the biggest story of her career.

Elena watches it air from the motel. Marco's face on television.
Holt's name on television. Meridian Analytics on television.

Her phone starts ringing. She doesn't recognize most of the numbers.
She answers one. It's a coastal emergency management coordinator.
He has questions. Professional ones. She answers them.

This is when the tide turns — not with a dramatic confrontation
but with a twenty-six-year-old and a local news crawl.


================================
BEAT 15
================================

FRONT:  The storm hits — and the coast was warned

BACK NOTES:
The storm makes landfall. Category 3. Exactly where Elena's models said.

Evacuation numbers are higher than they would have been. Not everyone got out.
But more did.

We don't see the storm directly. We see Elena watching a radar feed in the
motel room. We see the evacuation footage she's watching on her laptop.
We hear the wind outside her window — the outer band is passing over her.

End on her face. Not relief. Not vindication. Something quieter than that.
She knew. She said something. That's all she could do.
